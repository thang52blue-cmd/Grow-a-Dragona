- Preserve the gameplay rules and terminology defined here.
- Treat the server as authoritative for player data, economy, hatching, feeding, production, collection, selling, and plot upgrades.
- Keep Hatching Eggs and Production Eggs as separate item types.

## Core game loop

`Buy Hatching Egg → Hatch → Baby Dragon → Feed 4 times → Adult Dragon → Produce Production Egg → Collect → Sell for Gold → Buy another Hatching Egg`

## Item terminology

- **Hatching Egg**: purchased from the shop and opened to obtain a Dragon.
- **Production Egg**: generated by an Adult Dragon in its nest, collected by the player, and sold for Gold.
- Production Eggs must never be sent through the hatching system.
- Hatching Eggs and Production Eggs must have separate inventory keys, UI labels, transactions, and validation rules.

## Player and farm model

- Each player owns one farm plot.
- Starter farm: 2 Dragon display slots.
- The farm expands through paid upgrades up to 12 display slots.
- One slot holds one Dragon.
- Dragons placed on the farm are active/displayed; Dragons in Storage are not active for production or synergy.
- If all slots are occupied, newly hatched Dragons are moved to unlimited Storage instead of blocking the hatch.

## Section 1 — First session
The player's experience from joining the server for the very first time to holding their first dragon.
What this game is:
Grow a Dragona is an idle farm and collection game. Players hatch dragons from eggs, raise them from baby to adult, and collect the eggs those adult dragons produce over time. Those eggs are sold for gold, and gold is spent on more hatching eggs, giving players a chance at rarer, more valuable dragons. Dragons live in slots on the player's farm plot - they are placed there, not carried around - and sessions are designed to work both as very short check-ins and as longer, more involved sessions.
This section covers only the player's very first session: the moments between joining the game for the first time and having their first dragon growing on their farm.
Design approach for this session
The guiding rule for this feature is simple and easy over clever. A brand-new player's first minute should feel effortless and familiar, using patterns already proven in this genre, not novel ideas that need to be play-tested to find out if they work.
Player state at first join
* A small starter farm plot with 2 open slots
* 0 gold
* No inventory, no tutorial popups, no currency explainer
* One free egg, already sitting on the plot
Design intent: nothing pre-loaded dilutes the meaning of the first egg. The player's very first action in the game is the same action they'll repeat for the next hundred hours - opening an egg - so it should be front and center from frame one.
### UI Placeholder — Main HUD overlay
The persistent frame visible at all times once the player has anything to track: gold counter, egg basket icon and count (added once Section 7 is reached), and the Collection button (added once Section 12 is reached). Needs a layout that stays uncluttered when only gold exists, and scales gracefully as more elements are added.

  

	### UI Placeholder — Egg prompt (world prompt)
A simple, unmissable world-space prompt floating over any un-hatched egg, inviting a tap to hatch it. Needs a design for both this very first egg and every hatching egg bought later.

  

	How the farm plot works
The farm plot is a small grid of slots. A dragon that is hatched gets placed into an open slot automatically - it does not wander the plot and it does not follow the player around. This matches how the game already works everywhere else (adult dragons sit on a nest in their assigned slot), so a baby dragon behaves the same way from the moment it hatches, just without production yet.
* Starter plot: 2 slots, both empty at first join
* The hatched dragon is auto-assigned to the first open slot
* The player interacts with the dragon by walking up to its slot, the same way they will interact with every dragon afterward
What hatching an egg looks and feels like
Every egg in the game, free or paid, resolves through the same four beats:
* The egg shakes in place, building anticipation
* The egg cracks, and a flash of color hints at how rare the result is before the dragon is even visible
* The dragon reveals itself, scaling up into view
* The dragon is placed into the open slot automatically, confirmed by a brief, non-blocking summary card - never a required tap
For every hatch after the player's first one, a tap during the shake speeds the sequence up. For this very first hatch specifically, the skip is disabled - every new player sees the complete sequence once, at full length.

## Section 2 — Eggs
Covers both egg systems in the game: hatching eggs and production eggs. Mechanic and look-and-feel together.
Two completely different objects share the word "egg"
This game has two egg systems that must never be confused with each other, on screen or in the player's head:
* Hatching eggs - bought with gold from the shop. Opening one is how a player gets a new dragon. These are the gacha object.
* Production eggs - laid automatically by an adult dragon over time. They are collected and sold for gold. They are never opened and never produce a dragon.
A player who confuses these two has misunderstood the entire economy. Keeping their visual language, sound, and vocabulary clearly distinct is as important as any individual number below.

## Section 3 — Hatching
The full opening sequence for a hatching egg - every rarity, every time, not just the player's very first one.
The four beats
Every hatching egg, regardless of tier or the rarity it resolves to, plays the same four beats: the egg shakes in place, it cracks with a flash of color hinting at the result, the dragon reveals itself, and a short, non-blocking summary confirms the new dragon. What changes between rarities is how much each beat is dressed up - never the underlying structure.

	  Skip behavior
A player's very first hatch ever always plays at full length, not skippable, so every new player sees the complete sequence once. From the second hatch onward, tapping during the shake speeds that step down to about 0.3 seconds, regardless of what the egg is about to reveal - the player doesn't know the rarity yet at that point, so skip behavior can't depend on it. ## Section 4 — The dragon
The shared rules every dragon in the game follows, regardless of which specific dragon it is. Individual dragon designs (Ember the fire dragon, Aqua the water dragon, and so on) are unique art assets outside the scope of this document - what belongs here is the system layered on top of every one of them: life stage, element identity, and how a dragon behaves while sitting on the farm.
Rarity and element are pool categories, not a body color system
Rarity and element exist to organize the dragon pool - they drive which odds table an egg rolls against, which food type a dragon eats, and which synergy bonus it contributes. They are not a formula that recolors every dragon's body. Rarity's color language (white/grey, blue, purple, gold, rainbow) lives entirely in the egg shell and the hatch reveal flash, both already designed in earlier sections - it is never painted onto a living dragon. Each individual dragon has its own unique, hand-designed appearance, independent of any shared rarity palette.
Two life stages, one set of rules
Every dragon, whatever its unique design, follows the same two-stage structure:
Stage
	Behavior
	Visual
	Baby
	No production. Cannot be sold or moved from its slot. Grows only through feeding, per the flow already defined.
	Small, round, big-eyed, stubby-winged - deliberately the most helpless-looking version of any dragon
	Adult
	Produces eggs passively. Sits permanently in its assigned plot slot.
	Full size, fully detailed, full elemental effects active
	

A dragon never leaves its slot on its own, at either stage. The one exception in the entire game is a dragon equipped as a Mascota, which does follow the player - that is a separate system with its own design pass later, and does not change how every other dragon on the farm behaves.
Element identity - the layer that makes synergy readable
Because the game's synergy system rewards grouping same-element dragons together, a player needs to recognize a dragon's element at a glance, regardless of which specific dragon it is or how unique its individual design looks. That recognition comes from a consistent ambient effects layer applied on top of every dragon of that element, not from forcing every dragon of the same element to share a body color.
Not every one of the 42 dragons in the pool will have a bespoke animated idle behavior - that depends on each individual model's own animation budget. So this layer splits into two tiers: one guaranteed for every dragon regardless of its model, and one enhancement applied only where the model actually supports it.
Tier 1 - particle effects (every dragon, guaranteed)
Particles are emitted from a simple attachment point on the model rather than requiring any custom rig or animation, so this tier works identically on every dragon in the pool, no matter how simple or detailed its individual model is.

## Section 7 — Collecting
The interaction of taking eggs out of a nest and into the player's own carrying inventory. Turning those eggs into gold is a separate action, covered in its own section next - this one only covers getting eggs off the nest and into the player's hands.
Two steps, not one
Collecting does not sell an egg on the spot. It moves the egg into the player's personal egg inventory, which they carry around until they choose to visit their own market to sell it. This keeps collecting purely about clearing nests quickly, and gives selling its own distinct moment rather than burying it inside every single pickup.
The interaction
Walking up to a nest and tapping a single egg collects just that one - the default, quick, repeatable action. When 2 or more eggs are sitting in that same nest, a Collect all option becomes available and clears the whole nest in one action. Collect all only ever applies to the nest the player is currently standing at, not the whole farm at once - reaching a different dragon's nest still means walking over to it, same as every other interaction in the game.

## Section 12 — Storage
Where every dragon that doesn't fit on the plot lives, and how a player browses a collection that only ever grows. Nothing here changes how a dragon gets placed into a slot - that flow is already fully defined in Section 9 - this section covers what storage actually is and how a player looks through it.
Getting a dragon into storage
Storage fills up two ways, both already defined elsewhere: a newly hatched dragon overflows here automatically if every plot slot is already full (Section 3), and a player can manually send an already-placed dragon back here through the same slot-management option used to swap dragons (Section 9). There's no cost and no limit to how many dragons storage can hold - a player never has to make a hard choice about which dragons to keep versus release.
Two ways to open it
Storage isn't a physical building on the plot the way the market or food shop are - there's nothing to walk up to. It opens two ways instead, depending on what the player is trying to do:
* Contextually, through the same picker that already appears when tapping an empty or occupied plot slot (Section 9) - this is how a dragon actually gets placed
* Directly, through a Collection button in the HUD, accessible from anywhere on the plot - this opens the same list purely to browse, with no slot involved
The HUD version is view-only: a player can look through, admire, and check on their collection from anywhere, but actually moving a dragon onto the plot still means walking up to a slot and using its picker. This keeps placement itself tied to the same physical, walk-up interaction used for everything else on the farm, while still letting a player casually check their collection without needing to be near an empty slot to do it.
Browsing a growing collection
Unlike the simple running-total counter used for the egg inventory, dragons are individual, named collectibles - a player will want to actually look through them, not just see a number. The collection is shown as a grid of cards.
* Exact duplicates (same specific dragon, same rarity, same element) automatically group into a single card with a small count badge, rather than listing every copy separately - keeps a long collection readable instead of a wall of identical cards
* Each card shows a border in that dragon's rarity color (reusing the same rarity color language already established for egg shells and the hatch reveal flash), a small element icon, and whether it's currently a Baby or an Adult
* Default sort is by rarity, highest first, since seeing the best pulls up top matches the collection-pride feeling the game is going for. A toggle switches to newest-first for a player who wants to find something they just hatched

## Section 13 — Night raid
The risk-and-reward layer that makes leaving your own farm a real decision. Modeled closely on how Grow a Garden 2 handles its night stealing, adapted to this game's dragons and eggs.
Day and night cycle
Phase
	Duration
	What happens
	Day
	7.5 minutes
	Completely normal - no stealing is possible anywhere, regardless of who's standing where
	Dusk warning
	About 15 seconds
	The sky begins shifting color and a soft twilight chime plays - a heads-up that night is about to start, so a player who's out visiting can decide whether to head home
	Night
	2 minutes
	Stealing becomes possible on any unlocked farm (see below)
	

This cycle repeats continuously, synchronized for every player on the same server.
Locked vs. unlocked - the core rule
A farm is locked - completely safe, nothing can be stolen - whenever its owner is physically standing within their own plot boundary. The instant they step outside it, whether to visit someone else's farm, go raid another player, or just wander off, their own farm becomes unlocked and stays that way until they return, for as long as the current night phase lasts.
This is also how the game avoids ever needing to raid someone who has fully logged off: a farm only exists to be visited at all while its owner is connected to that same server. "Unlocked" only ever means "connected, but not currently standing at home" - never "offline."
* A locked farm shows a closed gate and a faint protective shimmer around its boundary, visible to anyone walking past
* An unlocked farm shows an open gate with no shimmer - readable at a glance by anyone deciding where to raid
Stealing - the raider's side
Walking onto an unlocked farm during night and approaching a nest lets a player steal one egg at a time - each grab takes a couple of seconds, noticeably slower and more deliberate than the owner's own instant collect. A stolen egg isn't actually the raider's yet: it has to be carried, on foot, all the way back to their own farm before it's safely banked into their own egg inventory, sellable exactly like anything they produced themselves.
Dark element synergy on the farm being raided (already defined in Section 9) applies here: the more Dark dragons that farm has in its display, the slower a raider moves while they're on it, from -10% at 2 Dark dragons up to -75% at a full set of 12. This is a passive defense that works even though the owner isn't there to actively do anything - exactly the payoff Dark synergy is meant to provide.
Defense - the broom
Every player carries a broom by default - no cost, nothing to unlock. If the original owner catches up to a raider anywhere while that raider is still carrying stolen eggs (hasn't made it back to bank them yet), one swing sends the raider stumbling back and instantly returns every egg they were currently carrying straight back to the exact nest they came from - full recovery, nothing partial. This mirrors the shovel in Grow a Garden 2 closely on purpose: it's a familiar, proven mechanic for exactly this kind of moment.
* Staying locked in your own farm is the simplest, guaranteed-safe choice for a given night phase - nothing can ever be stolen while you're home
* Leaving to go raid someone else is the only way to get eggs beyond what your own farm produces, but it means your own farm is exposed the whole time you're out
* Catching a raider in the act, anywhere, is the only way to recover eggs already in progress of being stolen - once they make it home, that egg is gone for good

## Section 14 — Mascota
A deliberately simple companion system: one dragon from the player's collection travels with them visually, without ever stopping what it was already doing on the farm.
Equipping
A player picks one dragon from their collection (the same browsing screen defined in Section 12) to equip as their Mascota. Only one can be equipped at a time - choosing a different dragon simply replaces the previous one. Equipping a dragon this way does not remove it from its display slot: it keeps producing and keeps counting toward element synergy exactly as if nothing changed. Mascota is an additional flag on a dragon, never a relocation.
### UI Placeholder — Mascota equip action
Not a new screen - an Equip as Mascota action added onto the existing Collection browse screen (Section 12), available on whichever dragon card the player selects.
	  How it follows the player
Rather than an independent companion with its own pathfinding and movement AI, the equipped dragon rides along with the player - attached to them like a backpack, moving exactly as the player moves. There's no separate logic to build or break: it reads as "your companion is with you" without any of the cost or fragility of a real independent follower, especially with many players and their mascots potentially visible in the same space.
The one benefit
Every other benefit this could have had - production boosts, egg quality boosts, gold bonuses - already lives somewhere else in the game (a dragon's own rarity, the variant odds table, the market). Stacking more bonuses onto Mascota on top of those would mostly double up on systems that already reward exactly the same thing. 

So Mascota keeps exactly one benefit, tied to the food shop already designed in Section 11: every time the player collects an egg, there's a chance their Mascota gifts them one free portion of its own element's food.
